Cannabis and Pets: Can CBD Help Your Dog or Cat?

March 17, 2026 9 min read

Learn about CBD for pets — what the research says, potential benefits for anxiety and pain, proper dosing by weight, THC toxicity risks, and how to choose safe pet CBD products.

As cannabis has become more mainstream and widely available, pet owners are increasingly curious about whether cannabinoids — particularly CBD — might benefit their furry companions. The pet CBD market has exploded in recent years, with products ranging from tinctures and treats to topical balms marketed for everything from anxiety relief to joint support. But behind the marketing hype lies an important and nuanced conversation about what is safe, what is effective, and what could be genuinely dangerous for your pets.

This guide provides an honest, science-based look at cannabis and pets. We will explore the research behind CBD for dogs and cats, explain why THC poses serious risks to animals, and give you practical guidance on dosing, product selection, and when to consult your veterinarian. Whether you are considering CBD for an aging dog with arthritis or a cat with anxiety, understanding the facts is essential for keeping your pet safe.

The endocannabinoid system in animals

Just like humans, dogs, cats, and virtually all vertebrate animals possess an endocannabinoid system (ECS). This biological system, discovered in the early 1990s, is a complex network of receptors, enzymes, and endogenous cannabinoids that plays a crucial role in regulating a wide range of physiological processes including pain perception, mood, appetite, immune function, and homeostasis. The fact that animals share this system with humans is the foundational reason why cannabinoids can affect them at all.

The ECS in animals contains the same two primary receptor types found in humans: CB1 receptors, which are concentrated in the brain and central nervous system, and CB2 receptors, found primarily in the immune system and peripheral organs. However, there is one critically important difference — dogs have a significantly higher concentration of CB1 receptors in their brains compared to humans. This means that compounds which bind to CB1 receptors, particularly THC, affect dogs much more intensely. What might produce a mild buzz in a human can cause severe neurological symptoms in a dog.

CBD interacts with the endocannabinoid system differently than THC. Rather than binding directly to CB1 receptors, CBD works indirectly by influencing the ECS to use its own endocannabinoids more effectively and by interacting with other receptor systems including serotonin receptors and vanilloid receptors involved in pain perception. This indirect mechanism is why CBD does not produce intoxication in either humans or animals and is generally considered to have a wider safety margin than THC.

Cats also have an endocannabinoid system, though research on feline responses to cannabinoids is less extensive than canine research. What we do know is that cats appear to metabolize cannabinoids differently than dogs, and they can be particularly sensitive to certain compounds. Cats lack some of the liver enzymes that help process certain substances, which is why they are famously sensitive to medications like aspirin and essential oils. This metabolic difference means that dosing for cats requires extra caution, and any CBD product used should be specifically formulated and tested for feline use.

Potential benefits of CBD for dogs and cats

The most robust research on CBD for pets has focused on dogs, with several well-designed clinical studies producing encouraging results. A landmark 2018 study at Cornell University's College of Veterinary Medicine found that CBD oil administered at 2 mg per kilogram of body weight twice daily significantly reduced pain and increased activity in dogs with osteoarthritis. Importantly, the study used objective veterinary assessments alongside owner reports, lending credibility to the findings. Dogs showed measurable improvements in comfort levels and mobility with no observable side effects at this dose.

Epilepsy is another area where CBD has shown particular promise for dogs. A 2019 study at Colorado State University demonstrated that 89 percent of dogs receiving CBD experienced a reduction in seizure frequency. While this was a small pilot study, the results were significant enough to spur larger follow-up research. Given that epilepsy affects an estimated 5 percent of all dogs and that many do not respond adequately to conventional anti-seizure medications, CBD represents a potentially valuable complementary treatment. It is worth noting that CBD has already received FDA approval for treating certain forms of epilepsy in humans through the drug Epidiolex.

Anxiety is perhaps the most common reason pet owners reach for CBD products. Dogs can suffer from separation anxiety, noise phobias such as fear of thunderstorms and fireworks, and generalized anxiety. While large-scale clinical trials on CBD for canine anxiety are still limited, anecdotal evidence from veterinarians and pet owners is abundant, and preliminary research suggests that CBD's interaction with serotonin receptors may produce genuine anxiolytic effects. Many pet owners report that their dogs are calmer during stressful events after receiving CBD, though individual responses vary considerably.

For cats, the research is less developed but growing. Anecdotal reports and early studies suggest that CBD may help with feline anxiety, inflammatory conditions, and age-related discomfort. A 2019 study at Cornell examined CBD pharmacokinetics in cats and found that it was absorbed and tolerated, though cats metabolized it differently than dogs. Some veterinarians are cautiously recommending CBD for cats with conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, chronic kidney disease-related nausea, and behavioral anxiety, though they emphasize the need for more research and careful product selection.

THC toxicity in pets and warning signs

While CBD appears to be relatively safe for pets, THC is a fundamentally different story. THC toxicity in dogs and cats is a serious veterinary concern that has increased dramatically as cannabis products have become more widely available. According to the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center, calls related to cannabis exposure in pets have risen substantially over the past decade. It is absolutely essential that pet owners understand the risks and know how to respond if accidental exposure occurs.

Dogs are particularly vulnerable to THC because of their higher density of CB1 receptors in the brain. Even small amounts of THC that would produce mild effects in a human can cause significant distress in a dog. Common symptoms of THC toxicity in dogs include severe lethargy and drowsiness, loss of coordination and stumbling (ataxia), urinary incontinence, dilated pupils, exaggerated startle response, vomiting, drooling, low heart rate, and hypothermia. In more severe cases, dogs may experience tremors, seizures, or fall into a stupor-like state that can last for many hours. While fatal outcomes are rare, they have been reported, particularly in very small dogs or when edibles containing chocolate or xylitol are involved, as these ingredients carry their own toxicity risks.

Cats are also at risk from THC exposure, though they tend to encounter cannabis products less frequently than dogs simply because they are generally less inclined to eat things indiscriminately. Symptoms of THC toxicity in cats are similar to those in dogs and include extreme lethargy, wobbling, hypersensitivity to touch and sound, dilated pupils, and in some cases, agitation and vocalization. Cats may also drool excessively and refuse food. Because cats metabolize cannabinoids differently, the effects can sometimes take longer to resolve.

If you suspect your pet has ingested a THC product, take action immediately. Contact your veterinarian or the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center (888-426-4435) right away. Be honest about what your pet consumed — veterinarians are not there to judge, and accurate information is critical for proper treatment. Treatment may include induced vomiting if ingestion was recent, activated charcoal to reduce absorption, IV fluids for hydration and temperature regulation, and monitoring in a quiet, dark environment to minimize sensory stimulation. Most dogs recover fully within 12 to 36 hours with appropriate supportive care, but the experience is frightening and distressing for both pet and owner.

Prevention is always better than treatment. Store all cannabis products — edibles, flower, concentrates, and tinctures — in secure, pet-proof containers kept well out of reach. Cannabis edibles are especially dangerous because they often taste appealing to dogs and may contain chocolate, which is independently toxic to canines. Never leave edibles unattended on counters, tables, or in bags that a curious pet could access. If you use cannabis at home, be mindful of where you place your products and treat them with the same care you would give any medication.

How to choose safe pet CBD products

The pet CBD market is currently unregulated, which means that product quality varies enormously. Some products contain exactly what they claim, while others may have significantly more or less CBD than labeled, or may contain contaminants including heavy metals, pesticides, or unwanted levels of THC. Choosing a safe, effective product requires careful evaluation, and cutting corners on quality is not worth the risk when your pet's health is at stake.

The most important thing to look for is a certificate of analysis (COA) from an independent, third-party laboratory. A legitimate COA will verify the cannabinoid content, confirming that the product contains the amount of CBD claimed on the label and that THC levels are below the legal limit of 0.3 percent — or ideally, non-detectable. The COA should also test for contaminants including heavy metals like lead and mercury, residual pesticides, residual solvents from the extraction process, and microbial contaminants like mold and bacteria. Reputable companies make their COAs easily accessible on their websites or via QR codes on their packaging.

Choose products specifically formulated for pets rather than repurposing human CBD products. Pet-specific formulations are dosed appropriately for animal body weights and typically use carrier oils that are safe for pets, such as MCT oil derived from coconut. Some human CBD products contain ingredients that may be safe for people but harmful to animals — for example, certain essential oils, artificial sweeteners like xylitol (which is extremely toxic to dogs), or flavorings that may not agree with an animal's digestive system. Pet CBD products also come in formats designed for easy administration, such as flavored tinctures, soft chews, and treats.

When evaluating brands, look for companies that are transparent about their sourcing and manufacturing processes. The best pet CBD companies use hemp grown in the United States under regulated conditions, employ clean extraction methods such as CO2 extraction, and follow good manufacturing practices (GMP). Customer reviews and veterinary endorsements can be helpful indicators of quality, though they should not replace your own due diligence. Be wary of companies that make specific medical claims about their products, as this is not permitted under FDA regulations and may indicate a less reputable operation.

Dosing CBD for pets by weight

Proper dosing is essential for both safety and effectiveness when giving CBD to your pet. Unlike human CBD use, where doses are relatively flexible and personal experimentation is common, pet dosing should be more precise because animals cannot communicate how they are feeling. The general approach mirrors what you have likely heard about cannabis edibles for humans: start low and go slow.

For dogs, most veterinary research and clinical guidelines suggest starting with 1 mg of CBD per kilogram of body weight, administered once or twice daily. This is a conservative starting point that allows you to observe your dog's response before increasing. If well-tolerated and more effect is desired, the dose can be gradually increased to 2 mg per kilogram twice daily, which is the dose that showed significant benefits in the Cornell arthritis study. Some conditions may eventually warrant higher doses, but increases should be made incrementally over the course of weeks, not days.

To put this in practical terms: a 10-pound (4.5 kg) dog would start with approximately 4.5 mg of CBD per dose. A 30-pound (13.6 kg) dog would start with about 13.6 mg. A 60-pound (27 kg) dog would begin at approximately 27 mg per dose. These are starting recommendations — your pet's individual response may require adjustments. Keep a simple log of the dose given, the time of administration, and any changes you observe in your pet's behavior, appetite, energy level, and symptoms. This log will be invaluable for your veterinarian and for fine-tuning the dose over time.

Dosing for cats requires extra caution due to their unique metabolism. Start with a lower dose of 0.5 mg per kilogram of body weight once daily and observe carefully. An average 10-pound (4.5 kg) cat would start with just 2.25 mg of CBD. Cats may metabolize CBD more slowly than dogs, so effects may take longer to appear and last longer. Increase the dose gradually and only if needed. Always use a product formulated specifically for cats, and consult your veterinarian before starting any CBD regimen, especially if your cat takes other medications, as CBD can interact with drugs metabolized by the same liver enzymes.

What veterinarians say about cannabis for pets

The veterinary community's stance on cannabis for pets has evolved significantly in recent years. While the profession was initially cautious to the point of silence, a growing body of research and increasing client demand have led many veterinarians to engage more openly with the topic. The American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA) has acknowledged the need for more research and has supported the removal of regulatory barriers that hinder clinical studies on cannabis in animals.

In several states, legislation has been passed or proposed to allow veterinarians to discuss and recommend CBD for their patients. California became the first state to pass such a law in 2018, and others have followed. These legal protections are important because, prior to such legislation, many veterinarians felt they could not openly discuss cannabis with pet owners without risking their professional licenses. The result was that pet owners were making decisions about cannabis for their pets based on internet advice rather than professional veterinary guidance.

Among veterinarians who are knowledgeable about cannabinoid medicine, the consensus is generally cautiously optimistic about CBD and clearly negative about THC for pets. Most will emphasize that while the early research on CBD for pain, epilepsy, and anxiety in dogs is encouraging, we are still in the relatively early stages of understanding long-term effects, optimal dosing protocols, and potential drug interactions. They typically recommend starting with the lowest effective dose, using only high-quality, lab-tested products, and maintaining regular check-ups including blood work to monitor liver function, since CBD is metabolized through the liver.

What virtually all veterinarians agree on is that THC should never be deliberately given to pets and that preventing accidental exposure is a critical responsibility for cannabis-using pet owners. They also caution against replacing proven veterinary treatments with CBD without professional consultation. CBD may complement conventional treatments, but it should not be viewed as a substitute for established medical therapies, particularly for serious conditions. If you are considering CBD for your pet, the best first step is always a conversation with your veterinarian, who can evaluate your pet's specific health situation and help you make an informed decision.
