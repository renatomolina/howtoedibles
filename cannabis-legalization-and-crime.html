How cannabis legalization has impacted crime rates

March 5, 2026 8 min read

Explore how cannabis legalization has impacted crime rates across the United States. Review the data on arrests, violent crime, and the broader effects on criminal justice.

Few arguments in the cannabis legalization debate have been as contentious as the relationship between legal marijuana and crime. Opponents long warned that loosening cannabis laws would lead to spikes in violent crime, property theft, and public safety hazards. Proponents countered that legalization would reduce the burden on criminal justice systems and redirect law enforcement resources toward more serious offenses. Now, with more than a decade of data from states like Colorado and Washington — and a growing number of states following suit — researchers have a much clearer picture of what actually happened.

The pre-legalization landscape

To understand the impact of legalization on crime, it helps to understand what came before it. For decades, cannabis prohibition was one of the primary drivers of drug arrests in the United States. In 2010, the FBI reported more than 850,000 marijuana-related arrests nationwide — more than all violent crime arrests combined. The vast majority of these arrests, roughly 88%, were for simple possession rather than distribution or manufacturing.

The enforcement of cannabis laws was also deeply unequal. A landmark 2013 report by the American Civil Liberties Union found that Black Americans were 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for marijuana possession than white Americans, despite roughly equal rates of use across racial groups. In some counties, the disparity was as high as 10 to 1. These arrests carried lasting consequences — criminal records that could block access to housing, employment, education, and public benefits for years or even decades after the fact.

The financial cost was staggering as well. The ACLU estimated that states collectively spent over $3.6 billion per year enforcing marijuana possession laws. Critics argued that these resources would be far better spent addressing violent crime, property crime, and other threats to public safety.

Crime rates after legalization

When Colorado and Washington became the first states to legalize recreational cannabis in 2012, opponents predicted a wave of crime. More than a decade later, the data tells a different story. A 2022 study published in the journal PLOS ONE analyzed crime data from 38 states over a 20-year period and found no evidence that recreational cannabis legalization increased violent crime or property crime. In fact, some legalized states experienced modest decreases in certain crime categories.

Research from Washington State University examined crime trends in Colorado, Washington, and Oregon before and after legalization and found no statistically significant increases in violent crime, burglary, or motor vehicle theft that could be attributed to cannabis legalization. A separate analysis by the Cato Institute reached similar conclusions, noting that dire predictions about legalization leading to crime waves had simply not materialized.

It is worth noting that crime trends are influenced by many factors — economic conditions, policing strategies, demographic shifts, and more. Isolating the effect of any single policy change is inherently difficult. However, the consistent finding across multiple studies and multiple states is clear: legalization has not been the crime catalyst that critics feared.

Impact on cannabis-related arrests

The most immediate and dramatic impact of legalization has been on cannabis-related arrests themselves. In Colorado, marijuana arrests fell by more than 95% following legalization. In Washington state, cannabis possession arrests dropped from over 5,500 per year before legalization to near zero afterward. Similar patterns have played out in every state that has legalized.

Nationally, the shift has been significant. FBI data shows that total marijuana arrests in the United States fell from approximately 750,000 in 2012 to under 170,000 by 2022 — a decline driven almost entirely by states adopting legalization or decriminalization measures. This represents hundreds of thousands of people each year who are no longer funneled into the criminal justice system for possessing a substance that a growing majority of Americans believe should be legal.

The reduction in arrests has also eased the burden on courts, jails, and public defenders. In many jurisdictions, cannabis cases once constituted a significant portion of the criminal docket. Removing these cases has allowed the justice system to focus its limited resources on more serious matters.

Violent crime and property crime

One of the most persistent concerns about legalization has been whether legal cannabis businesses would attract violent crime — robberies targeting cash-heavy dispensaries, for example, or turf wars over market share. While dispensary robberies have occurred in some areas, research suggests these incidents have not driven broader increases in violent crime.

A 2021 study published in the Journal of Economic Behavior and Organization found that the opening of recreational dispensaries was associated with a reduction in crime in surrounding neighborhoods, likely because dispensaries bring increased foot traffic, security infrastructure, and economic investment to previously underserved areas. The researchers compared neighborhoods that received dispensaries to similar neighborhoods that did not and found lower rates of both violent and property crime near the cannabis businesses.

The relationship between legalization and the illicit market also matters. By creating a regulated alternative to the black market, legalization has reduced the demand for illicit cannabis — and with it, the violence and criminal activity associated with underground drug trade. While the illicit market has not disappeared entirely, particularly in states with high taxes or overly restrictive licensing, the legal market has captured a significant share of cannabis sales and diminished the power of illegal operations.

Law enforcement resource reallocation

Beyond the direct impact on crime statistics, legalization has allowed law enforcement agencies to redirect their attention and budgets. Police departments in legalized states have reported that officers who previously spent significant time on marijuana enforcement — conducting searches, making arrests, processing evidence, and testifying in court — are now able to focus on violent crime, property crime, and community policing.

The fiscal benefits extend to the broader criminal justice system. States have saved millions of dollars in prosecution costs, incarceration expenses, and probation supervision by removing cannabis offenses from the criminal code. Meanwhile, cannabis tax revenue has generated billions of dollars that states have invested in education, public health, infrastructure, and — in some cases — programs specifically designed to repair the harms of the war on drugs.

Colorado, for instance, has collected over $2 billion in cannabis tax revenue since legalization, with portions allocated to school construction, substance abuse treatment, and youth prevention programs. Illinois directs a share of its cannabis tax revenue to a fund specifically designed to support communities most harmed by decades of disproportionate enforcement.

Social equity and expungement

Perhaps the most significant criminal justice impact of legalization has been the movement toward expunging past cannabis convictions. Recognizing that it would be unjust to allow some people to profit from legal cannabis while others remained burdened by criminal records for the same activity, many states have passed laws to clear past marijuana convictions.

Illinois became a national model when it passed the Cannabis Regulation and Tax Act in 2019, which included automatic expungement provisions for low-level cannabis offenses. California has dismissed or reduced more than 100,000 cannabis convictions under Proposition 64. New York's legalization law included provisions to automatically expunge records for offenses that are no longer crimes under the new law.

These expungement efforts have been life-changing for hundreds of thousands of individuals. A cannabis conviction can follow a person for life, affecting their ability to get a job, rent an apartment, access student loans, or even volunteer at their child's school. By clearing these records, states are acknowledging that the war on drugs imposed lasting harm on communities — particularly Black and Latino communities — and taking concrete steps to address that legacy.

Social equity programs in the cannabis industry itself are also part of this effort. States like Illinois, New York, and New Jersey have created licensing programs that prioritize applicants from communities disproportionately impacted by cannabis enforcement, offering reduced fees, technical assistance, and access to capital. While these programs have faced criticism for slow implementation, they represent an important recognition that legalization must address past harms, not just create new business opportunities.
