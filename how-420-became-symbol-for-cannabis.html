How 420 became the universal symbol for cannabis culture

March 5, 2026 7 min read

Discover the true origin of 420 and how it became the universal symbol for cannabis culture worldwide. From a group of California teens to a global phenomenon.

Few numbers carry as much cultural weight as 420. Mention it in almost any context and people immediately understand the reference. It appears on clocks, license plates, street signs, and price tags — always prompting a knowing smile from those in the cannabis community. But how did three simple digits become the universal shorthand for marijuana culture? The answer traces back to a small group of high school friends in 1970s California and a treasure map that never led to gold.

Key takeaway

The term 420 originated in 1971 with five California high school students called the Waldos, who met at 4:20 p.m. to search for an abandoned cannabis crop. The Grateful Dead community spread the code word worldwide, turning it into the most recognized symbol in cannabis culture.

The Waldos and the true origin story

In the fall of 1971, five students at San Rafael High School in Marin County, California, heard about a Coast Guard member who had planted a cannabis crop near the Point Reyes Peninsula but could no longer tend to it. The students — Steve Capper, Dave Reddix, Jeffrey Noel, Larry Schwartz, and Mark Gravitch — obtained a hand-drawn map supposedly showing the location of the abandoned plants. They called themselves "the Waldos" because their usual hangout spot was a wall outside the school.

The Waldos agreed to meet at the Louis Pasteur statue on campus at exactly 4:20 p.m., after sports practices ended, to begin their search. They would remind each other of their plans by saying "4:20 Louis" in the hallways. Week after week, the group piled into a car, smoked cannabis on the way, and searched the Point Reyes forest for the elusive crop. They never found it.

But something more lasting came from those failed expeditions. Over time, "4:20 Louis" was shortened to simply "4:20" — a discreet code the Waldos could use in front of parents, teachers, and anyone else without raising suspicion. It became their private shorthand for everything related to cannabis: the act of smoking, the substance itself, and the entire experience surrounding it.

Debunking common 420 myths

Over the decades, dozens of alternative origin stories have circulated, and nearly all of them are false. One of the most persistent claims is that 420 is a police radio code for marijuana use in progress. In reality, California penal code section 420 relates to obstructing entry on public land, and no major law enforcement agency has ever used 420 as a dispatch code for cannabis offenses.

Another popular myth claims that there are 420 chemical compounds in cannabis. While cannabis contains hundreds of identified compounds — including cannabinoids, terpenes, and flavonoids — the actual number has never been precisely 420, and this figure has no basis in botanical science.

Some have tried to link 420 to Bob Dylan's song "Rainy Day Women #12 & 35," pointing out that 12 multiplied by 35 equals 420. While the song's famous refrain does reference getting "stoned," this mathematical coincidence is just that — a coincidence. Other debunked theories include connections to Adolf Hitler's birthday (April 20, 1889), the number of a bill in Congress, or tea time in Holland. The Waldos have provided extensive documentation — including postmarked letters, a 420 flag, and contemporaneous references — that definitively trace the term to their group.

The Grateful Dead connection

The term might have remained an inside joke among a handful of friends in Marin County if not for a crucial connection to one of the most influential bands in American counterculture. Several of the Waldos had personal ties to the Grateful Dead. Mark Gravitch's father managed the band's real estate, and Dave Reddix's older brother was close friends with Dead bassist Phil Lesh. Reddix himself eventually became a roadie for the band.

Through these connections, the term 420 entered the Grateful Dead's inner circle during the early 1970s. The Dead's famously cannabis-friendly touring community — the Deadheads — adopted the phrase with enthusiasm. As the band toured relentlessly across the country throughout the 1970s, 1980s, and into the 1990s, Deadheads carried the term with them from city to city, show to show. In the parking lots surrounding every Grateful Dead concert, 420 became common vocabulary.

The Deadhead community functioned as a remarkably effective distribution network for the term. These were dedicated fans who traveled in groups, built tight-knit communities, and shared a culture that was deeply intertwined with cannabis use. By the late 1980s, 420 had spread well beyond the Grateful Dead scene and into the broader counterculture.

420 goes mainstream

The leap from subcultural slang to mainstream awareness came in 1991 when a group of Deadheads in Oakland, California, distributed flyers inviting people to smoke cannabis on April 20th at 4:20 p.m. One of these flyers made its way to Steve Bloom, a reporter for High Times magazine. Bloom published the flyer in the magazine in 1991, and High Times began using 420 extensively in its coverage.

As the premier cannabis publication of the era, High Times had enormous influence over marijuana culture. The magazine embraced 420 wholeheartedly, referencing it in articles, advertisements, and event promotions. Through High Times, the term reached cannabis enthusiasts who had no connection to the Waldos or the Grateful Dead. By the mid-1990s, 420 had become the universally recognized code for cannabis.

The rise of the internet in the late 1990s and early 2000s accelerated the term's spread even further. Online forums, chat rooms, and early social media platforms allowed cannabis enthusiasts worldwide to discover and adopt 420 as part of their shared vocabulary. It appeared in movies, television shows, and music — always carrying its unmistakable connotation.

April 20th celebrations worldwide

What began as a time on the clock gradually transformed into a date on the calendar. April 20th — written as 4/20 in the American date format — has become the unofficial cannabis holiday, celebrated with gatherings, festivals, and demonstrations around the world.

Some of the largest annual celebrations take place at Hippie Hill in San Francisco's Golden Gate Park, where tens of thousands gather each April 20th. Denver's Civic Center Park hosts a massive 420 festival that draws crowds exceeding 50,000. In Vancouver, the annual 420 event at Sunset Beach has become one of Canada's largest cannabis gatherings. London's Hyde Park, Melbourne's Flagstaff Gardens, and dozens of other locations worldwide hold their own April 20th observances.

These events serve a dual purpose. For many attendees, they are celebrations of cannabis culture — a chance to gather with like-minded people and enjoy the plant openly. But 420 celebrations have also historically functioned as political demonstrations, drawing attention to the push for legalization and reform. Many of the largest annual gatherings began as protests against prohibition and continue to advocate for policy changes even in jurisdictions where cannabis has been legalized.

420 in the modern legalization era

As cannabis legalization has swept across North America and gained traction globally, the meaning of 420 has continued to evolve. What was once a covert code used to evade detection has become a mainstream cultural reference and a powerful marketing tool. Licensed dispensaries run 420 sales and promotions. Cannabis brands incorporate the number into their product names, logos, and pricing. In Colorado, the official highway marker for Route 420 had to be replaced with "Mile 419.99" because the original sign was stolen so frequently.

The cannabis industry has fully embraced April 20th as its biggest commercial event of the year, comparable to Black Friday for retail or Super Bowl Sunday for advertising. Dispensaries report that April 20th is consistently their highest-grossing day, with sales often doubling or tripling compared to an average day.

Yet 420 has not lost its countercultural edge entirely. In states and countries where cannabis remains illegal, the term still functions as a symbol of resistance and solidarity. Advocacy organizations continue to use April 20th as a rallying point for legalization efforts. The number connects a 55-year lineage from five teenagers searching for a hidden cannabis patch in the California hills to a global movement that has reshaped drug policy in dozens of jurisdictions.

The story of 420 is ultimately a story about how culture spreads. A private joke between friends became a subcultural code word, then a counterculture rallying cry, and finally a mainstream symbol recognized around the world. It required no marketing budget, no corporate campaign, and no official endorsement — just a group of friends, a treasure hunt that went nowhere, and the right connections to carry the message forward. More than half a century later, 4:20 still means exactly what the Waldos intended it to mean: it is time.
